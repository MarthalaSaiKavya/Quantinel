Layer 1

Data & News

Daily Returns
ret[t] = (close[t] / close[t-1]) - 1

Why: Every downstream metric derives from this. Percentage change keeps all stocks comparable regardless of price level — a $10 move in a $500 stock is very different from a $10 move in a $15 stock.

Sentiment Score
score = (pos_words - neg_words) / (n_articles × 2) clamped to [-1.0, +1.0]

Why: Markets react to narrative, not just price. A simple keyword bag (beat/surge/miss/warn…) gives a fast, auditable signal without a heavy NLP model. Dividing by 2 × article count prevents a single hyper-bullish headline from dominating.

Sentiment Aggregation
avg_sentiment[ticker] = mean(scores in last 5 days)

Why: A rolling 5-day window aligns with the 5-day rebalance and forecast horizon. Older news has already been priced in; very recent articles carry more weight by being in a smaller pool.

Feeds into → Layer 3 (Markov sub-agent uses per-ticker sentiment to shift the bull/bear transition matrix) and Layer 6 (raw headlines surfaced in the dashboard).
Layer 2

Forecast

Expected Return
exp_ret = mean(last 20 daily rets) × horizon_days factor_scores = U × S momentum = F[-1,0] - F[-H,0] signal = momentum × Vt[0, ticker_i] exp_ret = signal × vol × √H

Why two methods: Momentum is the dumb baseline — hard to beat over short windows. The quantum path decomposes the return matrix into market factors via SVD, reads the dominant factor's recent trend, and projects each ticker's exposure to that trend forward. A ticker that loads heavily on a rising factor should move more.

Direction (+1 / −1)
direction[t] = sign(expected_return[t])

Why: The optimizer needs to know long vs short, not just magnitude. Isolating direction makes it possible to evaluate directional accuracy independently in Layer 6 — a useful diagnostic when the magnitude estimate is noisy but the sign is right.

Confidence (0–1)
conf = min(1, |mean_ret| / std_ret) conf = min(1, |factor_momentum| / factor_vol)

Why: A Sharpe-like signal-to-noise ratio on the forecast itself. A large expected return in a high-volatility regime is less trustworthy than the same return in a calm one. Confidence is not yet used by the optimizer but is available for future weighting.

Feeds into → Layer 3 GBM (uses expected_returns as the drift in path simulation) and Layer 4 (all optimizers take expected_returns as the mu vector). Layer 6 checks direction against actual returns for Directional Accuracy.
Layer 3

Risk

Annualised Covariance
Σ = rets.cov() × 252 vol[t] = √Σ[t,t]

Why ×252: Annualising puts the covariance on the same scale as annualised expected returns, so the Markowitz objective μ·w − λw'Σw in Layer 4 balances the two terms correctly.

VaR 95%
VaR = percentile(simulated_returns, 5)

Why 5th percentile: The 95% one-tailed VaR asks "what is the worst outcome we'd expect to see at most 5% of the time?" It is the standard risk-desk threshold — auditors and quant judges recognise it immediately. Expressed as a negative return (e.g. −0.07 = could lose 7%).

CVaR 95%
CVaR = mean(returns ≤ VaR)

Why CVaR over VaR alone: VaR says "loss won't exceed X 95% of the time" but says nothing about how bad the other 5% is. CVaR (Expected Shortfall) averages the tail — it penalises fat tails and is the metric Basel III uses for bank capital.

Three sub-agents each produce independent VaR and CVaR estimates, then the ensemble aggregates them.

GBM
Parametric simulation
mu_d = exp_ret / H sig_d = annual_vol / √252 ret = mu_d + sig_d × Z VaR = pct(cumprod_ret, 5)

Why: Uses forecast drift — the only sub-agent that ties the risk estimate to the forward view. Clean, fast, Gaussian. Its weakness is fat-tail blindness.

Markov
Regime-switching
regime = (ret ≥ median) → bull/bear P adjusted by news sentiment simulate path via regime transitions VaR = pct(path_returns, 5)

Why: Markets have two moods. When sentiment is negative, we nudge the bear regime stickier — the only place where news directly changes a risk number.

Bootstrap
Non-parametric
blocks = chunks of history, len≈H/2 resample blocks → horizon path VaR = pct(path_returns, 5)

Why: No distribution assumption. Block-resampling preserves autocorrelation and naturally captures crash-like fat tails that GBM misses.

Aggregated VaR & CVaR
var_95 = median(gbm, markov, bootstrap) cvar_95 = min(gbm, markov, bootstrap)

Why median for VaR: Avoids any single model dominating — the middle-of-the-road estimate. Why min (worst) for CVaR: When the tail is bad, we take the most conservative view. CVaR is our downside protection number.

Disagreement [0, ∞)
for each ticker: dev = |var_agent - median_var| / |median_var| disagreement = mean(all devs)

Why: When GBM, Markov, and Bootstrap give very different VaR numbers, no single model should be trusted. Disagreement quantifies this uncertainty. It is passed directly to the optimizer as a position-size shrinkage factor.

Feeds into → Layer 4 optimizer receives cov, var_95, cvar_95, and disagreement. The disagreement scalar directly shrinks weights — it is the primary cross-layer uncertainty signal.
Layer 4

Optimize

Markowitz Objective
maximise μ·w − λ·w'Σw solution: w ∝ Σ⁻¹·μ

Why this form: The first term rewards expected return; the second term penalises variance. λ (risk aversion = 8) balances them. The closed-form inverse means no iterative solver needed — fast and deterministic.

Dollar Neutral & Gross Scale
w = w − mean(w) → sum(w) ≈ 0 w = w / sum(|w|) → gross = 1

Why dollar neutral: Removes market beta — we're trying to profit from relative NVDA vs GOOG calls, not a bet that the market goes up. Gross = 1 means 50 cents long, 50 cents short per dollar of capital.

Disagreement Shrinkage
shrink = 1 / (1 + disagreement) weights = weights × shrink

Why: This is the feedback loop from Layer 3 into Layer 4. High model disagreement → shrink factor closer to 0 → smaller bets. It is a principled way to translate epistemic uncertainty into caution without changing the optimizer's logic.

QUBO Objective (Quantum)
Q = λΣ − diag(μ) minimise w'Qw ground state = eigvec(Q, min eigval) w = sign(ground_state)

Why QUBO: Discrete long/short picks are a combinatorial problem — the same structure QAOA/VQE solve on a real QPU. By casting Markowitz as minimising w'Qw we get a problem the quantum hardware is designed for.

Feeds into → Layer 5 receives TargetPortfolio.weights. Layer 6 computes PnL as Σ(weight × forward_return) and charges turnover costs when weights change.
Layer 5

Execute

Realized Weights
shares = int((w × capital) / price) realized_w = (shares × price) / capital

Why whole-share rounding: Real brokers execute in whole shares. The gap between target and realized weights is the first taste of execution friction — it shows up as tracking error in Layer 6's PnL calculation.

Fill Records
Fill(ticker, side, qty, price) side = "buy" if shares > 0

Why track fills: Provides an audit trail for every position change. When we switch to live execution (AlpacaExecutor), the Fill objects become real order receipts — same structure, no downstream changes needed.

Feeds into → Layer 6 uses realized_weights (not target weights) to compute PnL, so the score reflects actual execution, not ideal weights.
Layer 6

Score

Metric Formula Why it matters
Sharpe Ratio mean(rets) / std(rets) × √52 Risk-adjusted return, annualised. The single most universal quant benchmark. √52 annualises weekly rebalances. Sharpe > 1 is good; > 2 is excellent.
Total Return cumprod(1 + rets) − 1 Raw compounded P&L over the backtest window. Judges and investors understand it immediately even without quant background.
Directional Accuracy count(sign(direction) == sign(actual)) / total Did the forecast call up/down correctly? 50% = coin flip. Directly grades the Layer 2 direction signal in isolation from magnitude noise.
Information Coefficient pearson_r(predicted_returns, actual_returns) Gold standard for alpha research. IC = 0 means the forecast is useless; IC = 0.05–0.10 is considered good in practice. Unlike DA, it penalises wrong magnitudes too.
vs. Baseline Sharpe strategy_sharpe − baseline_sharpe Baseline is 50/50 equal-weight buy-and-hold. Positive = the selected pipeline earns its complexity. This is the model-arena benchmark for baseline vs challenger.
Period PnL Σ(w × fwd_ret) − turnover × 0.0002 2 bps transaction cost is charged on every unit of weight change (turnover). Without this, a hyperactive optimizer looks great on paper but loses to fees in reality.
VaR Breach Rate
breach = (actual_ret < −VaR) breach_rate = breaches / total_checks expected = 0.05 (for 95% VaR)

Why: A perfectly calibrated VaR should be violated exactly 5% of the time. If the breach rate is much higher, the risk model is too optimistic. Much lower means it is over-conservative and the optimizer is holding back too much. Checked per sub-agent to identify which model is best calibrated.

Avg / Max Disagreement
avg_dis = mean(disagreement over rebalances) max_dis = peak(disagreement)

Why: Tracks how often the three risk models diverged over the whole backtest. High avg disagreement means the risk model was consistently uncertain — and weights were consistently shrunk. It explains low gross exposure in stressed periods.

This is the deliverable. Scorecard + RiskReport together answer: did the pipeline make money (Sharpe, total return), did it make smarter bets than random (IC, DA), did it beat doing nothing (vs. baseline), and did the risk model know what it was doing (VaR breach rate)?
